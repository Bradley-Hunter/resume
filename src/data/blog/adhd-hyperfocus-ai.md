When hyperfocus hits, I'm not just coding. I'm inside the problem. The rest of the world mutes itself. I'm not hungry, not tired, not aware that my back has been screaming at me for the last two hours. There's only the next line, the next fix, the next feature. For me, that state is where my best work happens. It's also incredibly fragile.

## The AI Promise

AI coding tools are supposed to make you faster. For an ADHD brain, faster sounds like the dream. Less friction between the idea and the implementation. Less time fighting boilerplate, less time looking up syntax I've seen a hundred times. In theory, it keeps me in the flow longer.

For a while, I believed that. I still mostly do. But I've run into a problem I didn't anticipate.

## The Review Problem

When AI writes a block of code, it's not my code yet. I didn't think through every line. That means I have to stop and actually read it. Understand it. Make sure it does what I think it does.

That sounds reasonable. It is reasonable. But for me, that stop is a trapdoor.

Hyperfocus works by narrowing attention to a single thread. I'm not just thinking about the current function. I'm holding the next three things I need to do in my head, the reason I'm doing them, the context behind the context. When I stop to read AI-generated code, I'm not just pausing. I'm asking my brain to switch modes. Review mode and build mode don't coexist well for me. One of them wins.

## What Actually Happens

Usually one of two things happens when I break focus to review AI output.

The more common one for me is a sudden awareness of my body. All at once I notice that I'm hungry, that I need to use the bathroom, that my neck is stiff, that my eyes are dry. These signals were there the whole time. I just couldn't perceive them while I was deep in flow. The moment hyperfocus breaks, they all arrive at once.

The other outcome, not far behind, is a boredom cascade. I lose the thread of what I was building, why it mattered, what came next. And once that thread is gone, so is the pull. The momentum disappears. I close the editor and end up doing something else entirely, one of my hobbies, something low-stakes that my brain can engage with without the context it just lost.

Neither of these are a great outcome when I was in the middle of building something.

## What Helps (A Little)

I want to be clear: these are things that work for me. I'm not offering a protocol.

The most reliable thing I've found is physically writing down what I was about to do next before I stop to review anything. A sticky note with one line: what is the next thing I need to implement after this. Not a digital note, not a comment in the code. Actual pen on paper. Something that exists in my physical space and doesn't disappear when I switch windows. When review breaks my flow, that note is a handhold I can grab to pull myself back.

The other thing is intentional breaks. Before I go too deep, before I get three hours in and fully unreachable, stopping deliberately. Checking in. Taking a breath.

This one is harder. By definition, the deeper I am in hyperfocus, the less aware I am that I should take a break. The state that makes breaks most useful is the same state that makes me least likely to remember to take one. I don't have a clean solution for that. I'm still working on it.

What I do know is that AI tools are here to stay, and for neurodivergent programmers they come with tradeoffs that don't get talked about much. The speed is real. The review cost is also real. For my brain specifically, understanding that tension has been more useful than pretending it doesn't exist.
